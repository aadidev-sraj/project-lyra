Phishing Detection

Master the art of identifying and defending against deceptive phishing attacks.

Understanding Phishing

Phishing is a cybercrime in which a target or targets are contacted by email, telephone, or text message by someone posing as a legitimate institution to lure individuals into providing sensitive data such as personally identifiable information, banking and credit card details, and passwords. The information is then used to access important accounts and can result in identity theft and financial loss.

Deception

Phishing relies on tricking victims into believing a communication is legitimate.

Information Theft

The primary goal is to steal sensitive personal or financial data.

Consequences

Can lead to identity theft, financial fraud, and system compromise.

Common Attack Vectors

Email Phishing

Critical

The most common form, using fake emails to impersonate trusted entities like banks, social media, or government agencies.

Red Flags:

  • Suspicious sender address
  • Generic greetings
  • Urgent or threatening language
  • Links to fake websites

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

High

Phishing attempts delivered via text messages, often containing malicious links or requests for personal information.

Red Flags:

  • Unexpected messages
  • Links to unknown sites
  • Requests for immediate action
  • Grammatical errors

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Medium

Using phone calls to trick victims into revealing sensitive information, often impersonating tech support or law enforcement.

Red Flags:

  • Unsolicited calls
  • Requests for remote access
  • Pressure to act quickly
  • Threats of legal action

Real-World Case Studies

2016

Democratic National Committee (DNC) Hack

Political

Russian state-sponsored hackers used spear-phishing emails to gain access to the DNC's computer networks. The emails contained malicious links that, when clicked, prompted users to change their passwords on a fake Google login page.

Lessons Learned:
  • Even high-profile targets are vulnerable.
  • Spear-phishing is highly effective.
  • Importance of scrutinizing login pages.

2017

Google & Facebook Phishing Scam

Financial

A Lithuanian man defrauded Google and Facebook of over $100 million by sending fake invoices to employees, impersonating a legitimate Asian manufacturer. This was a sophisticated business email compromise (BEC) scam.

Lessons Learned:
  • Verify payment requests through multiple channels.
  • Beware of subtle email address spoofing.
  • Financial departments are prime targets.

2020

COVID-19 Themed Phishing Attacks

Severe

Cybercriminals heavily leveraged the global pandemic, sending phishing emails disguised as official communications from health organizations (WHO, CDC) or government relief programs to distribute malware or steal credentials.

Lessons Learned:
  • Attackers exploit current events and crises.
  • Always verify sources for sensitive information.
  • Be wary of unsolicited health or financial advice.

Defense Strategies

Awareness & Verification

Inspect Sender Details

Always check the sender's full email address, not just the display name. Look for misspellings or unusual domains.

Hover Over Links

Before clicking, hover your mouse over any links to see the actual URL. If it doesn't match the expected domain, it's likely malicious.

Verify Independently

If an email or message seems urgent, contact the organization directly using official contact information (not from the suspicious message).

Technical Safeguards

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds an extra layer of security, making it much harder for attackers to access your accounts even if they steal your password.

Regular Data Backups

In case of a successful attack (e.g., ransomware), having recent backups can help restore your data without paying a ransom.

Use Antivirus & Firewalls

Keep your security software updated. These tools can detect and block malicious software often delivered via phishing.
